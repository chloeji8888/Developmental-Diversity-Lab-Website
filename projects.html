CURRENT PROJECTS

OCognitive Behavioral Therapy for Youth with Autism & Emotional / Behavioral Needs in Community Based Setting (CYAN)

We are conducting a research study that aims to improve the well-being of children aged 6-14 years with autism and emotional dysregulation, such as irritability and anxiety. The study involves comparing cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) to usual clinical care. The CBT manual used in this study (MEYA) has been well-supported by previous research and is freely accessible on our website. It provides mental health clinicians with user-friendly digital and print materials to help them conduct therapy sessions effectively. By participating in this research study, mental health clinicians have the opportunity to contribute to the development and refinement of effective interventions for children with autism and emotional dysregulation. The study aims to determine the benefits of CBT in real-world clinical settings and improve training procedures for mental health professionals working with individuals on the autism spectrum. Objective and Personalized Assessments of Youth Support Needs and Intervention Outcomes

Objective and personalized assessments of youth support needs and intervention outcomes are crucial for tailoring interventions to individual needs and understanding the diverse behavioral patterns associated with autism, fostering a comprehensive and effective approach to support and intervention strategies. The measures described, namely the Children’s Household-Environment Engagement Rating Scale (CHEERS), Autism-Related Characteristics Home Evaluation Rating (ARCHER) Scale, Youth Top Problems (YTP) Symptom Categories, and the YTP interview, share a common transdiagnostic theme focused on assessing and understanding various aspects of children's behavior and engagement within the home environment. The CHEERS evaluates a child’s engagement levels based on previous research and consultation with experts, aiming to provide independent evaluators with a tool to code child engagement in parent-recorded home videos pre- and post-intervention. ARCHER, on the other hand, measures common autism-related behaviors in the home environment, utilizing observations without standardized prompts. The YTP Symptom Categories encompass a broad range of behavioral and symptomatic patterns associated with autism, reported by caregivers. Finally, the YTP interview is a comprehensive one-hour session that aims to identify personalized symptoms and treatment goals for children with autism across various mental health settings, emphasizing a transdiagnostic approach to understanding and addressing the heterogeneity of autism in functional domains. These measures collectively contribute to a holistic understanding of children's behaviors, engaging in a transdiagnostic exploration that goes beyond specific diagnostic categories.

Empathy Processes in Childhood and Adolescence

When children exhibit appropriate responses to others in distress, this plays a positive role in their social functioning and helping behaviors. Autistic individuals may face challenges with the ability to discriminate between various emotional states and how to respond (Conner et al., 2018; Mazefsky & White, 2014). The study examined the relationship between self-regulation and empathy in school-age children (ages 7-13 years) with autism. This study utilized quantitative measures of self-regulation and empathy to analyze the association between factors of self-regulation and component-based domains of empathy. Results highlighted a significant association between the valence of an emotion (e.g., positive, or negative emotions) and levels of empathy. This work may inform future studies on the impact of self-regulation in children with autism as they reach school age.

Gender and Neurodiversity

Autism diagnosis accuracy and occurrence varies significantly between males and females, identifying individuals at a ratio of 1 female to 4 males (Kirkovki, 2013). However, little is known about how diagnostic accuracy and experiences for autistic individuals differ based on gender identity and the barriers that keep one from receiving an initial diagnosis based on their gender identity. Four papers were written to examine this using mixed methodology. A quantitative exploration using the National Database of Autism Research found that out of the 5,000 children in the database, female consistently scored lower than males in every domain on the ADOS-II. Additionally, factory analyses revealed that the restrictive and repetitive questions were more strongly related to each factor in the male group. Secondly, qualitative research probed participants’ experiences with autism in relation to their gender identities through a series of interviews. Within 27 participants, themes of gender-differentiated symptom presentation and clinician bias revealed how gender socialization impacted females to camouflage their symptoms and how the stereotype of autism caused bias from families and clinicians. Lastly, these findings revealed that timing of gender diverse identities and autistic identity formations coincided and were linear, as the social difficulties reported by our sample included a lack of understanding of gendered themes.

Transition to Adulthood in Youth on the Autism Spectrum

With many teens having to transition from a mainly educational system of support to a set of health and social service systems (Shattuck et al., 2017), there is a critical need to advance research and support services in the area of autism and transition to aid autistic individuals and their families. A series of interviews of autistic adults and stakeholders has been conducted to illuminate the struggles this population experiences during this time of transition and what changes could be made to the existing systems in place to improve outcomes in adulthood.

Development of Friendship in Schools and Online

Creators of social skills interventions (SSIs) for autistic youth often have the goal of teaching children how to act in different social situations with ultimate goals of “making friends, learning from others and developing hobbies,” of which many in the autistic advocate community dislike the pushing to adhere to neurotypical norms. As such, this research line explores how SSIs may not be aligned with the social goals of autistic youth. A systematic review of the recent 27 SSIs found that the social validity of their outcome measures was questionable and that the majority of interventions did not use the input of autistic advocates. With limited knowledge about what the social goals of autistic youth are, this series of research examines how autistic youth develop social resilience and what fulfills their social needs. A first examination found that within an online community of both neurotypical and neurodivergent individuals, those who have closer virtual friends reported higher rates of loneliness and higher rates of friendship closeness, compared to those with closer in-person friends. Secondly, a qualitative study interviewing autistic youth provides in-depth information about friendship formation, social needs, and social resilience.

Past Projects

MEYA: Supporting Practitioners Working with Youth on the Autism Spectrum

The development and implementation of Modular Evidence-Based Practices for Youth with Autism (MEYA) represents a pivotal advancement in clinical training and decision-making for professionals working with youth on the spectrum. MEYA, stemming from the SEBASTIEN program, which evolved from Behavioral Interventions for Anxiety in Children with Autism (BIACA), focuses on establishing strong alliances with patients and their families to create a collaborative and supportive environment. Through evidence-based interventions, MEYA aims to enhance self-regulation, promote adaptive behaviors, and address the diverse spectrum of personality traits, symptom expressions, and psychiatric comorbidities observed in children on the spectrum.

i. This comprehensive approach is exemplified in TAASD (Treatment for Anxiety in Autism Spectrum Disorders), a three-site randomized controlled trial investigating the efficacy of a modular Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) protocol for anxiety in children on the spectrum compared to standard CBT and treatment-as-usual control. Additionally, SEBASTIAN, detailed in two papers, emphasizes modular cognitive-behavioral therapy for autism-related symptoms and personalized autism symptom assessment using the Youth Top Problems Scale. The overarching integration of evidence-based practices and personalized approaches showcased in MEYA and its predecessors contributes significantly to advancing the understanding and treatment of autism in diverse clinical settings.

ii. The CBT for Youth with Autism & Emotional/Behavioral Needs (CYAN) study plays a vital role in enhancing the well-being of children aged 6-14 years with autism and emotional dysregulation. This research, comparing cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) to usual clinical care, focuses on improving therapeutic interventions for this population. The CBT manual employed in the study, known as MEYA, is well-supported by previous research and is readily accessible on the website, providing mental health clinicians with valuable resources for effective therapy sessions. Participating mental health clinicians not only contribute to the development and refinement of interventions for children on the spectrum and emotional dysregulation but also help determine the real-world benefits of CBT in various community mental health settings. This study aims to advance training procedures for mental health professionals working with individuals on the spectrum, contributing to the ongoing effort to enhance the quality of care for this diverse and often underserved population.

BIACA

BIACA is an example of an adapted CBT treatment approach. The modular format of adapted CBT allows for components of treatment to be apportioned based on need giving an opportunity for a personalized treatment program for supporting the reduction of a child’s anxiety and autism-related struggles.

Evidence-Based Interventions for Autistic Youth

In developing psychological intervention programs for autistic youth, our research focus began with anxiety as a primary emphasis (Sze & Wood, 2007, 2008; Wood et al., 2009a) and then expanded to also include social and other important foci (Wood et al., 2009b, 2021, 2022, 2024). Results from our 3-site clinical trial of a 16-week intervention program entitled Behavioral Interventions for Anxiety in Children on the Autism Spectrum (BIACA; Wood et al., 2020) suggest that for outcomes ranging from anxiety to social responsiveness, BIACA outperformed both standard CBT as well as treatment-as-usual across multiple measures (Wood et al., 2020). Similar findings on BIACA have been reported by an independent research group (Storch et al., 2013, 2015). We then developed an expanded evidence-based intervention program, Schema-, Emotion, and Behavior-Focused Therapy for Children (SEBASTIEN; meya.ucla.edu), comprehensively addressing family-defined high priority clinical needs across most psychological and behavioral domains (mental health, social engagement, friendship, flexibility, and self-care) in a systematic fashion. SEBASTIEN uses measurement-based care and a modular design to personalize intervention for each child and family. It has also been designed for implementation in community care settings (see next section) and has been the focus of two randomized clinical trials (e.g., Wood et al., 2014; 2021; 2022). In these studies, independent ratings of youth social engagement during school recess and during 10 hours of videotaped daily life at home per family, as well as parent ratings of “youth top problems” over the course of intervention, were used to evaluate the outcomes of SEBASTIEN, and across these measures, SEBASTIEN consistently outperformed a group therapy condition focusing on social skills and self-regulation.

Implementation of Evidence-Based Practices for Autistic Youth in Community Settings

Individuals with fewer initial resources (e.g., many children attending urban schools) tend to receive fewer services even when they have a high level of mental health need. We have written about the prospects for school-based mental health services to overcome these systemic barriers, irrespective of family background (e.g., Langer et al., 2015; Wood et al., 2008). We have developed a free internet-based practitioner training protocol entitled MEYA (Modular EBPs for Youth on the Autism Spectrum) to facilitate implementation of evidence-based interventions for autistic youth being served in community settings (https://meya.ucla.edu). The training program appears to support practitioner adherence and competence in evidence-based practices (Wood et al., 2024). We are currently engaged in research funded by the United States Department of Defense Autism Research Program to study the effectiveness of this training program and its constituent therapy/counseling practices in California service settings such as public schools, community mental health clinics, regional centers, and military health centers.

Assessment of Social Engagement, Mental Health, and Other Clinical Needs in Youth on the Autism Spectrum

Some of our earlier work focused on the construct validity of clinical anxiety in the context of autism (e.g., Wood & Gadow, 2010; Lecavalier, Wood, et al., 2014; Renno & Wood, 2013). More recent work has emphasized personalized assessment of clinical needs in youth on the spectrum and the use of video-based observation methods in the home setting to assess social engagement and parent-child relationships in families of autistic youth. Expanding beyond traditional questionnaire measures with limited scope, mixed evidence of validity, and sometimes marginal relevance to intervention planning has been the underlying emphasis of this line of research. We have found that it is possible for caregivers to define clinical goals for their autistic children across multiple domains (mental health, social engagement, friendship, flexibility, and self-care) in a way that is efficient, psychometrically valid, and sensitive to psychosocial intervention (e.g., BIACA, SEBASTIEN, as noted above; e.g., Wood et al., 2022). Equally importantly, such measures can be used to help practitioners and families prioritize intervention emphasis and track treatment response efficiently to assist in determining when to shift the focus of treatment (e.g., when to shift the focus from self-regulation skills to friendship development) (Wood et al., 2024). This type of measurement-based care is a central element to the MEYA training program mentioned above (https://meya.ucla.edu), giving practitioners a tool for selecting intervention foci and for receiving automated guidance on shifting from one clinical emphasis to another.

Cognitive Behavioral Interventions for Anxiety, OCD, and Depression

Children with clinically impairing anxiety have been characterized as having several ecological risk factors that are potentially malleable via interventions: (a) specific patterns of parent-child interactions that may inadvertently support clinical anxiety, and (b) social difficulties with peers that may serve to maintain clinical anxiety as a result of stress, stigma, and poor social self-efficacy. While first generation cognitive behavioral intervention programs targeted children’s emotion regulation and attention biases towards threat cues, less attention was focused directly on ecological risk factors. It was suspected that addressing the environment in which children became anxious might enhance treatment effects. This program of research began with a literature review (Wood et al., 2003) and meta-analysis (McLeod, Wood, & Weisz, 2004) showing that intrusive and rejecting parenting were linked with anxiety symptoms in children, as well as descriptive and longitudinal research documenting similar trends with measures of parenting developed in our lab (e.g., Wood, 2006; Wood et al., 2004, 2007) which have been used in replications and extensions of this research program by others in ensuing years (e.g., Raudino et al., 2013). We developed a cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) program to augment standard CBT practices with an explicit focus on intrusive and rejecting parenting and positive relationships with peers (Wood & McLeod, 2008), and in an RCT, compared it to a standard-of-care CBT program for childhood anxiety, finding that the augmented program outperformed the standard-of-care in terms of independent evaluators’ ratings of treatment response and anxiety severity at posttreatment and 1-year followup (Wood et al., 2006, 2009), and that reductions in parental intrusiveness during treatment mediated the children’s improved clinical status at 1-year-follow-up (Wood et al., 2009).

Emotion Regulation, Social Adjustment, and Schooling

Mental health challenges have implications for youth adjustment in numerous domains, and one of the most consequential of the potential effects is in the domain of schooling—both academic performance and school attendance. We have completed several studies examining linkages between anxiety (and emotion regulation), peer acceptance and friendship, and academic outcomes and school attendance (e.g., Wood et al., 2012; Wood, 2006, 2007; Galla, Wood et al., 2014). These studies have demonstrated linkages between anxiety and close relationships with peers, and between anxiety (and emotion regulation) and school adjustment and success, among children and adolescents. Several of these are especially noteworthy due to their longitudinal design (e.g., Wood et al., 2012—published in Child Development; Wood, 2006—published in Developmental Psychology). In these studies, generally using panel designs and two or more timepoints of data collected with intervals of months or years, a number of important findings emerged at the within and between person levels. For example, the more that anxiety decreased (or capacity for emotion regulation increased) over time for a child, the more the child’s academic performance tended to increase in these studies. A similar pattern of findings was identified for social adjustment. Previous studies in the literature have often relied on concurrent assessments of these constructs to provide support for the hypothesis that anxiety impairs academic and social adjustment, but have been unable to test for dynamic associations over time. Children with clinically impairing anxiety also often experience significant school avoidance (e.g., Wood, 2006). To better understand the relation between anxiety, emotion regulation, and attendance, we developed a conceptual model of the determinants and consequences of attendance problems among youth in urban schools (e.g., Wood, 2007). In a longitudinal study conducted on three separate national datasets, high levels of absenteeism in a previous year yielded a higher probability of meeting clinical criteria for externalizing and internalizing problems in the following year for youth attending US middle schools using a cross-lagged regression design (Wood et al., 2012). Based on these findings and others, it appears that a bi-directional relationship between mental health needs and school absenteeism can develop for youth, with implications for preventive intervention.
